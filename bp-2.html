Introduction

I had actually sketched a rough proof of my claim $($but had not done the construction$)$ like $9-10$ months ago but I don't know why I felt like writing a formal solution after a long time, lmao. It's a cute problem overall, with difficulty umm, I'd say d$3$-ish. I came across this problem in one of the blogs by Shreeansh Hota, so he had written ISI UG-B last year and had shared the solutions on his blog. The basic idea in his solution and mine are pretty much the same accept for the construction style $($he didn't give a construction, he actually had an increment argument$)$.

Problem

$($ ISI UG-B, $2023$ $)$ Determine all integers $n>1$ such that every power of $n$ has an odd number of digits.

Solution

For convenience call a number $n\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge1}$ froggy if every power of $n$ has an odd number of digits in base $10$ representation. We claim that a natural $n$ is froggy if and only if it's of the form $\fbox{$10^{\ell}$}$ where $\ell$ is a natural and $2\lvert\ell$.

The backward directional statement of the claim is trivial so we don't pay much attention to that. To prove the other direction, we assume that $n$ is not a power of $10$. So for $n$ to be called froggy, it has to satisfy the following inequality for any choice of $k\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 1}$; $$10^{2m} < n^k < 10^{2m+1}; \quad \text{where $m\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 1}$}$$ Taking the logarithm base $10$ throughout the inequality we obtain the following; $$2m < k\cdot\log n < 2m+1$$

Now, a clever or rather useful way to rewrite $k\cdot\log n$ would be to write in terms of the floor function and fractional part $($you'll appreciate it later$)$ and that is; $$2m < k\lfloor \log n\rfloor + k\{\log n\} < 2m+1$$ The key observation is to see that $\lfloor \log n\rfloor$ is even so our goal will be to construct a $k$ such that $1 < k\{\log n\}< 2$. The next step is a very natural one and that is to divide the latter inequality by $\{\log n\}$ throughout and we obtain the following key result; $$\cfrac{1}{\{\log n\}}< k< \cfrac{2}{\{\log n\}}$$

But, how do we confirm the existence of such $k$? It's surprisingly simple! Consider the distance between the points $1 /\{\log n\}$ and $2/\{\log n\}$ which is always more than $1$ that's because $\{\}:\mathbb{R}\to [0,1).$ Hence $n$ is not froggy. For a rigorous proof of the existence of an integer in an interval with a size more than $1$, consider the below lemma.

Lemma 1. Consider an open interval $(a,b)$ in $\mathbb{R}$ $($it could be open or closed, doesn't really matter$)$, if the size of the interval is more than $1$ then there exists an integer in the interval.
Proof. Note that there is a number $r\in(a,b)$ such that $r+1\in(a,b)$. A simple construction of an integer in the interval would be $\lfloor r+1\rfloor$. $\square$
Recall the fact that, $x-1< \lfloor x\rfloor \le x$ for any choice of $x\in\mathbb{R}$. So, we can peacefully conclude that our initial claim is true. $\square$

Remarks

If we are interested in investigaing the least power of $n\ne 10^{\ell}$ such that $n$ has even number of digits, we have a nice upper bound that is $2/\{\log n\}$.
